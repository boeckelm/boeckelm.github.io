COMMUNICATIONS ENGINEERING

Chapter 1 Introduction

1.1 What is Communications Engineering?

Definition 1.1 (Communications) In technical areas, telecommunications (German: Telekommunikationstechnik) or short communications (German: Kommunikationstechnik) defines the unaltered transmission of information over a channel.

Depending on whether more theoretical basics or practical implementations are considered, the terms communications theory, communications systems, and communications engineering are also used (in German also Nachrichtenübertragungstechnik or Übertragungstechnik).

Another area within communications is information theory (German: Informationstheorie) which has been founded by Claude E. Shannon (1948) (see Appendix A.9). This theory quantifies the limits of transmission of information over a channel. Please see Chapter 7 for further details!

1.3 Signals in Communications

1.3.1 Signals and Their Spectra

Definition 1.2 (Signal) A signal (German: Signal) in communications represents a message by a physical quantity like, e.g., a time-variant voltage \(u(t)\) or an electric field strength \(E(t)\). For the sake of simplicity, we abbreviate signals as \(x(t)\) and choose the unit to be \([x(t)]=1\sqrt{\text{W}}\) (see also Appendix C).1

Besides representing signals over time, it can be sometimes necessary to represent them over frequency, resulting in the so-called spectrum (German: Spektrum) of a signal. Here, the spectrum of a deterministic time-continuous signal \(x(t)\) is defined via its Fourier transform (German: Fouriertransformation) \[X(f) = \int\limits_{-\infty}^\infty x(t) e^{-j2\pi f t} dt,\] and the one of stochastic time-continuous signals2 \(x(t)\) via the Power Spectral Density (PSD, German: Leistungsdichtespektrum) \[\Phi_x(f) \leftrightarrow \varphi_x(t)=E[x(t)x(t+\tau)]= \lim_{T\to\infty}\frac{1}{T}\int\limits_{-\frac{T}{2}}^\frac{T}{2} x(t)x(t+\tau)dt.\] For more details on the Fourier transform or PSD, cf. Appendix E.1 or Appendix E.4, respectively. Deterministic and stochastic signals will be defined in the next subsection, i.e., Subsection 1.3.2.

Example 1.1 (Signal Spectra) The spectrum of an analog phone signal lies within the range of 300 Hz to 3.4 kHz, whereas a general audio signal contains frequencies in the range of 15 Hz to 20 kHz. The bandwidth of a digital Long-Term Evolution (LTE) signal can be up to 20 MHz and its center frequency depends on the operator as well as the area where it is deployed (rural or urban).

1.3.2 Deterministic and Stochastic Signals

Here and in the next subsection, we discuss different classifications of signals. One possible classification is to distinguish between deterministic and stochastic signals.

Definition 1.3 (Deterministic Signal) A deterministic (German: deterministisch) signal has a known characteristic and can be represented by a function.

Thus, deterministic signals contain no information in the communications sense.

Example 1.2 (Deterministic Signal) An example of a deterministic signal is a carrier signal, i.e., e.g., \(u(t) = 1\,\text{V} \cdot \cos(2.4\,\text{GHz} \cdot 2\pi t)\) as the carrier of ISM band devices.
Definition 1.4 (Stochastic Signal) A stochastic (German: stochastisch) signal (or process) has a random characteristic and can be only represented by statistics like, e.g., its mean, its auto-correlation function, or its PSD (see Appendix E.4 for more details).
Example 1.3 (Stochastic Signal) Figure 1.1 shows a band-limited white Gaussian noise signal \(x(t)\) as an example for a stochastic signal together with an estimate \(\hat{\Phi}_x(f)\) of its PSD. The one-sided bandwidth of the signal is \(B=4\,\text{kHz}\) and the noise power density is assumed to be \(N_0=10^{-5}\,\text{W}/\text{Hz}\). This results in a noise power of \(P_n=\sigma_n^2=BN_0=40\,\text{mW}\). More details of noise signals and explanations to the above mentioned parameters can be found in Section 4.1.1. With the following tool, one can listen to the audio file representing the considered stochastic signal.

Figure 1.1: Bandlimited white Gaussian noise signal and a cut-out of its two-sided PSD estimate

Stochastic signals play a major role in modeling, investigating, and optimizing the components of a communications system.

Remark. The transmission of information is modeled as a stochastic process (signal) because at the receiver, the information is not a priori known. Besides, impairments like, e.g., noise and interference, are stochastic processes (signals).

1.3.3 Continuous and Discrete Signals

Another classification of signals is to separate continuous and discrete signals. Note that the time domain and/or the value domain can be discrete or continuous, leading to four types of signals which are summarised in Figure 1.2.

Figure 1.2: Different types of communications signals

Finally, when we talk about analog signals, we refer to signals which are time-and value-continuous and used for analog communications whereas we use the term digital signals for time-and value-discrete signals used for digital communications. Although this course focuses solely on digital communications, we require analog signals when transmitting digital data over an analog channel as we will see in Chapter 3.

1.3.4 Digital Representation

In digital communications, signals are

  • represented digitally (time- and value-discrete, pulse-code modulated),
  • compressed and coded (e.g., for error correction) and mixed with addresses,
  • but finally transmitted with analog signals (pulses) which are matched to the analog channel.

More details to the digital representation of analog signals will be presented in Chapter 2.

1.4 Communications Systems

1.4.1 Wired and Wireless Systems

Communications systems consist of a network (German: Netzwerk) of two or, in the most cases, of several nodes (German: Knoten) or transceivers (German: Sendeempfänger), i.e., devices containing transmitters and receivers, which are connected with each other via a communications link. This link can be either a wire or a wireless over-the-air channel.

In wired (German: drahtgebunden) communications systems, network nodes are connected via wires, i.e., either copper or optical fibre. Examples of wired communications systems are:

  • Digital Subscriber Line (DSL),
  • Integrated Services Digital Network (ISDN),
  • Fibre To The Home (FTTH),
  • Ethernet, IEEE 802.3, and
  • Universal Serial Bus (USB).

Wireless (German: drahtlos) communications systems transmit information over the air, i.e., no wired connections between network nodes are required. There exists principally two categories of wireless systems, viz., systems operating in unlicensed (German: unlinzensiert) frequency bands and systems operating in licensed (German: lizensiert) frequency bands. Examples of unlicensed wireless systems are:

  • Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN), IEEE 802.11, Wi-Fi,
  • Worldwide Interoperability for Microwave Access (WiMAX), IEEE 802.16 (used also in licensed spectra), and
  • Bluetooth.

Examples of licensed wireless systems are:

  • Global System for Mobile Communications (GSM, 2nd generation mobile system) / Enhanced Data Rates for GSM Evolution (EDGE),
  • Universal Mobile Telecommunications System (UMTS, 3rd generation mobile system) / High Speed Packet Access (HSPA),
  • Long-Term Evolution (LTE, 4th generation mobile system), LTE-Advanced, LTE-Advanced Pro, and
  • New Radio (NR, 5th generation mobile system).

1.4.2 Frequency Bands

The so-called frequency plan (German: Frequenzplan or Frequenzbelegungsplan) of a country defines the assignment of frequency bands, i.e., a frequency region, to certain communications systems with specific usage rules. In Germany, this frequency plan is defined by the Bundesnetzagentur (Bundesnetzagentur 2019) and can be found here.

Exercise 1.1 (Signal Classification in a Communications System) Consider the digital communications system of Figure 1.3.

  1. Determine for each of the signals in the block diagram, viz., \(x(t)\), \(x[n]\), \(b[k]\), \(u[k]\), \(s(t)\), \(r(t)\), \(\hat{u}[k]\), \(\hat{b}[k]\), \(\hat{x}[n]\), and \(\hat{x}(t)\), if the signal is time-continuous, time-discrete, value-continuous, value-discrete, analog and/or digital.
  2. Explain why a digital communications system finally transmits the digital signal in an analog way? Why don’t we just transmit the analog signal directly over the channel?

Solution (Signal Classification in a Communications System). The solution to Exercise 1.1 is as follows:

  1. \(x(t)\), \(s(t)\), \(r(t)\), \(\hat{x}(t)\): analog, i.e., time- and value-continuous (German: zeit- und wertkontinuierlich
    \(x[n]\), \(\hat{x}[n]\): time-discrete (German: zeitdiskret) and value-continuous
    \(b[k]\), \(\hat{b}[k]\), \(u[k]\), \(\hat{u}[k]\): digital, i.e., time- and value-discrete (German: wertdiskret)

  2. Transforming the analog signal \(x(t)\) to the digital signal \(b[k]\) paves the way for source and channel coding (German: Quellen- und Kanalcodierung), i.e., it is possible to insert error correction coding which improves the performance of the system. Moreover, modulation can construct the analog signal \(s(t)\) from the digital coded signal \(u[k]\) in a specific way taking the physical channel into account. We will see in later sections that the transmitted signal \(s(t)\) is matched to the channel, again leading to an improved performance of the system.

  1. The reason to choose the unit \(\sqrt{\text{W}}\) for signals is that the instantaneous power (deutsch: Momentanleistung) of a signal \(x(t)\) is defined as \(p(t)=x^2(t)\). If we consider the voltage \(u(t)\) or current \(i(t)\) of a resistor with the resistance \(R\), the signal \(x(t)\) is defined as: \(x(t)=u(t)/\sqrt{R}\) or \(x(t)=i(t)\sqrt{R}\) and the instantaneous power computes as usual to \(p(t)=u^2(t)/R=Ri^2(t)\).↩︎

  2. For the sake of simplicity, we use the notation \(x(t)\) for the random process as well as its realisation. It should be clear from the context if it is the one or the other.↩︎

  3. Round brackets denote time-continuous and squared brackets time-discrete signals!↩︎
