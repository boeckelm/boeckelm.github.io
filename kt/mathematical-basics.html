E Mathematical Basics

E.1 Fourier Transform

Definition E.1 (Fourier Transform) The Fourier transform (German: Fouriertransformation) of a signal \(x(t)\) is defined as52 \[X(f) = \int\limits_{-\infty}^\infty x(t) e^{-j2\pi f t} dt\] and the inverse Fourier transform as \[x(t) = \int\limits_{-\infty}^\infty X(f) e^{j2\pi f t} df.\] In the following, this relationship will be abbreviated by \(x(t) \leftrightarrow X(f)\).

Table E.1 summarises the most important Fourier transforms used in communications engineering (see also Appendix E.2 for the definition of the Dirac delta function and Appendix D.1 for the definition of the rectangular function \(\mathrm{rect}(t)\)).

 Table E.1: List of important Fourier transform pairs53
Signal \(x(t)\) Spectrum \(X(f)\)
\(\delta(t)\) \(1\)
\(\sin(2\pi f_0 t)\) \(\frac{j}{2}\delta(f+f_0) - \frac{j}{2}\delta(f-f_0)\)
\(\cos(2\pi f_0 t)\) \(\frac{1}{2}\delta(f+f_0) + \frac{1}{2}\delta(f-f_0)\)
\(\mathrm{rect}(t)\) \(\frac{\sin(\pi f)}{\pi f}=:\mathrm{sinc}(f)\)

Theorem E.1 summarises the most important properties of the Fourier transform. For a proof of these properties, we refer to, e.g., Papula (2014) or Bronstein et al. (1997).

Theorem E.1 (Properties of the Fourier Transform) With \(\alpha,\alpha_1,\alpha_2\in\mathbb{R}\), \(x(t) \leftrightarrow X(f)\), and \(x_{1/2}(t) \leftrightarrow X_{1/2}(f)\):

  • Linearity: \(\alpha_1 x_1(t) + \alpha_2 x_2(t) \leftrightarrow \alpha_1 X_1(f) + \alpha_2 X_2(f)\)
  • Duality: \(X(t) \leftrightarrow x(-f)\)
  • Scaling: \(x(\alpha t) \leftrightarrow \frac{1}{|\alpha|} X\left( \frac{f}{\alpha} \right)\)
  • Time Translation: \(x(t-t_0) \leftrightarrow X(f)e^{-j2\pi f t_0}\)
  • Frequency Translation: \(x(t)e^{j2\pi f_0 t} \leftrightarrow X(f-f_0)\)
  • Symmetry: \(x(t)\in\mathbb{R} \leftrightarrow X(f)\) where
    • \(|X(-f)|=|X(f)|\) and \(\angle X(-f) = - \angle X(f)\)
    • \(\mathrm{Re}\{X(-f)\} = \mathrm{Re}\{X(f)\}\) and \(\mathrm{Im}\{X(-f)\}=-\mathrm{Im}\{X(f)\}\)
  • Multiplication: \(x_1(t)\cdot x_2(t) \leftrightarrow X_1(f) * X_2(f)\)
  • Convolution: \(x_1(t)*x_2(t) \leftrightarrow X_1(f)\cdot X_2(f)\) (see also Appendix E.3)
  • Conjugation: \(x^*(t) \leftrightarrow X^*(-f)\)

Exercise E.1 (Fourier Transform) Compute the Fourier transform \(X(f)\) of the following signals and draw the signals as well as the absolute value of their spectra, i.e., \(|X(f)|\), in a diagram by using a Computer Algebra System (CAS) of your choice. If you don’t have a CAS at your hand, a qualitative plot is sufficient.

    Figure E.1: Signal \(x(t)\) and spectrum \(X(f)=|X(f)|\)

  2. It holds (see Table E.1): \[\cos(2\pi f_0 t) \leftrightarrow \frac{1}{2}\delta(f+f_0) + \frac{1}{2}\delta(f-f_0)\] and due to the scaling property of the Fourier transform (see Theorem E.1) \[\mathrm{rect}\left(t\frac{f_0}{4}\right) \leftrightarrow \frac{4}{|f_0|}\mathrm{sinc}\left(\frac{4f}{f_0}\right).\] Multiplying two functions in the time domain yields convolution in the frequency domain (see again Theorem E.1), i.e., \[\cos(2\pi f_0 t)\cdot\mathrm{rect}\left(t\frac{f_0}{4}\right)\] \[\updownarrow\] \[\left(\frac{1}{2}\delta(f+f_0) + \frac{1}{2}\delta(f-f_0)\right)*\frac{4}{|f_0|}\mathrm{sinc}\left(\frac{4f}{f_0}\right).\] Finally, we get with \(f_0>0\) \[x(t) \leftrightarrow \frac{2\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}}{f_0}\left(\mathrm{sinc}\left(\frac{4(f+f_0)}{f_0}\right)+\mathrm{sinc}\left(\frac{4(f-f_0)}{f_0}\right)\right).\] Figure E.2 shows the signal \(x(t)\) and the absolute value \(|X(f)|\) of its spectrum, respectively.

E.2 Dirac Delta Function

E.2.1 Definition and Properties

Definition E.2 (Dirac Delta Function) The unitless Dirac delta function is defined via the following limit \[\delta(x) := \lim_{\varepsilon\to 0} \frac{1}{\varepsilon}\mathrm{rect}{\frac{x}{\varepsilon}},\] where \(\mathrm{rect}(\cdot)\) denotes the rectangular function as introduced in Appendix D.1 and \([\varepsilon]=1\). In the time and frequency domain, the Dirac delta function is defined as \[\begin{aligned}\delta(t) &:= \lim_{\varepsilon_t\to 0} \frac{1}{\varepsilon_t}\mathrm{rect}{\frac{t}{\varepsilon_t}}\ \text{and}\\ \delta(f) &:= \lim_{\varepsilon_f\to 0} \frac{1}{\varepsilon_f}\mathrm{rect}{\frac{f}{\varepsilon_f}}, \end{aligned}\] respectively. Here, the units \([\varepsilon_t]=1\,\mathrm{s}\) and \([\varepsilon_f]=1\,\mathrm{Hz}\).

Note that due to the scaling property of the Dirac delta function, i.e., \(\delta(\alpha x)=\delta(x)/|\alpha|\) (see, e.g., Papula (2014) or Wikipedia (2020) and references therein), the unit of the Dirac delta function is the reciprocal value of the unit of its argument. This leads to the fact that the unit of the Dirac delta function in the time domain is \([\delta(t)]=1\,\mathrm{s}^{-1}\) and in the frequency domain \([\delta(f)]=1\,\mathrm{Hz}^{-1}\) (see also Appendix C).

Figures E.4 and E.5 illustrate the series of rectangular functions in the time and frequency domain for decreasing \(\varepsilon_t\) and \(\varepsilon_f\), respectively. Thus, the Dirac delta function describes a very short impulse with infinite amplitude at \(t=0\) but where the area below the impulse is limited to one (see Theorem E.2).

Figure E.4: Series of rectangular functions in the time domain resulting in the Dirac delta function \(\delta(t)\)

Figure E.5: Series of rectangular functions in the frequency domain resulting in the Dirac delta function \(\delta(f)\)

Note that in Figures E.4 and E.5, the ordinates of the Dirac delta function \(\delta(t)\) and \(\delta(f)\) have no unit although the units \([\delta(t)]=1\,\mathrm{s}^{-1}\) and \([\delta(f)]=1\,\mathrm{Hz}^{-1}\), respectively. This is no typo and arises from the fact that the Dirac delta function is drawn as a vector whose length corresponds to the area underneath it and the unit of the area is \([\delta(t)dt]=[\delta(f)df]=1\) in the given case. This special notation makes it difficult to draw finite signals in the same diagram as signals with Diracs, e.g., a sampled signal as defined in Subsection 2.1. Although they have the same unit, the representation is different because on the one hand, the signal itself, and on the other, an area below the signal is plotted.

Theorem E.2 summarises the most important properties of the Dirac delta function required for understanding of some derivations in this textbook. For derivations of these properties see, e.g., Papula (2014) or Wikipedia (2020).

Theorem E.2 (Most Important Properties of the Dirac Delta Function) The Dirac delta function \(\delta(t)\) has the following properties:

  • Area: \(\int\limits_{-\infty}^\infty \delta(t) dt = 1\),
  • Multiplication: \(x(t)\delta(t-t_0)=x(t_0)\delta(t-t_0)\),
  • Convolution: \(x(t)*\delta(t-t_0)=x(t-t_0)\) (“\(*\)” denotes convolution, see Appendix E.3), and
  • Fourier transform: \(\delta(t)\leftrightarrow 1\).
These properties hold also for \(\delta(f)\) or \(\delta(x)\) by substituting \(t\) by \(f\) or \(t\) by \(x\), respectively.

E.2.2 Dirac Comb

Theorem E.3 (Dirac Comb) The Fourier transform of a Dirac comb, i.e., a sequence of Dirac delta functions, is given by \[\begin{equation} T_\mathrm{s}\sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty \delta(t-nT_\mathrm{s}) \leftrightarrow \sum_{k=-\infty}^\infty \delta(f - k f_\mathrm{s}), \tag{E.1} \end{equation}\] where \(f_\mathrm{s} = 1/T_\mathrm{s}\).

Figure E.6 shows a Dirac comb signal together with its spectrum.

Figure E.6: Dirac comb in the time and frequency domain

Proof. In order to prove Theorem E.3, we rewrite the left and right hand side individually and show that the results are identical. Let us first consider the left hand side of Eq. (E.1). Review Appendix E.1 in order to understand the next steps. The Fourier transform of a Dirac is a constant function, i.e., \(\delta(t) \leftrightarrow \Delta(f)=1\). Shifting the Dirac delta function by \(nT_\mathrm{s}\) to the right, yields a phase shift in the frequency domain. Precisely speaking, \(\delta(t-nT_\mathrm{s})\leftrightarrow e^{-j2\pi nT_\mathrm{s}f}\). Thus, the left hand side of Eq. (E.1) can be rewritten as \[\begin{equation} T_\mathrm{s}\sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty \delta(t-nT_\mathrm{s}) \leftrightarrow T_\mathrm{s}\sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty e^{-j2\pi nT_\mathrm{s}f}. \tag{E.2} \end{equation}\] Next, we consider the Fourier series (German: Fourier-Reihe, Papula (2014)) of the right hand side of Eq. (E.1). The Fourier series exists because the right hand is a periodic function where \(f_\mathrm{s}\) is the period.54 Therefore, it holds \[\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=-\infty}^\infty \delta(f - k f_\mathrm{s}) = \sum_{\ell=-\infty}^\infty c_\ell e^{j2\pi \ell f / f_\mathrm{s}}, \end{equation*}\] with the Fourier coefficients \[\begin{align*} c_\ell &= \frac{1}{f_\mathrm{s}}\int\limits_{-f_\mathrm{s}/2}^{f_\mathrm{s}/2} \sum_{k=-\infty}^\infty \delta(f - k f_\mathrm{s}) e^{-j2\pi \ell f / f_\mathrm{s}} df \\ &= \frac{1}{f_\mathrm{s}}\int\limits_{-f_\mathrm{s}/2}^{f_\mathrm{s}/2} \delta(f) e^{-j2\pi \ell f / f_\mathrm{s}} df = \frac{1}{f_\mathrm{s}}\underbrace{e^{-j2\pi \ell 0 / f_\mathrm{s}}}_{=1} \underbrace{\int\limits_{-f_\mathrm{s}/2}^{f_\mathrm{s}/2} \delta(f) df}_{=1} = \frac{1}{f_\mathrm{s}}. \end{align*}\] With \(\ell=-n\) and the fact that \(f_\mathrm{s}=1/T_\mathrm{s}\), the right hand side of Eq. (E.1) gets finally \[\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=-\infty}^\infty \delta(f - k f_\mathrm{s}) = \frac{1}{f_\mathrm{s}}\sum_{\ell=-\infty}^\infty e^{j2\pi \ell f / f_\mathrm{s}} =T_\mathrm{s} \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty e^{-j2\pi n f T_\mathrm{s}}, \end{equation*}\] which is the same as the left hand side of Eq. (E.1) as rewritten in Eq. (E.2) and the proof of Theorem E.3 is complete.

E.3 Convolution

Definition E.3 (Convolution) The convolution of the two functions \(x_1(t)\) and \(x_2(t)\) is defined as55 \[x_1(t) * x_2(t) = \int\limits_{-\infty}^\infty x_1(\tau)x_2^*(t-\tau)d\tau,\] with the unit \([x_1(t)*x_2(t)]=[x_1(t)]\cdot[x_2(t)]\cdot 1\,\mathrm{s}\).56

The major application of convolution lies in system theory. There, convolution is used to compute the output of a Linear Time-Invariant (LTI) system in terms of its input. To do so, the impulse response \(h(t)\), i.e., the output of the system in case of the Dirac delta function \(\delta(t)\) being its input, is used. It holds \[\begin{equation*} y(t) = h(t) * x(t) \leftrightarrow Y(f) = H(f)\cdot X(f), \end{equation*}\] where \(H(f)\) is the frequency response of the system, i.e., \(h(t)\leftrightarrow H(f)\). Note that the unit of the impulse response is \([h(t)]=1\,\mathrm{s}^{-1}\) (see also Appendix C). Thus, if \([x(t)]=1\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\), the unit of the system output is \([y(t)]=[x(t)]\cdot[h(t)]\cdot 1\,\mathrm{s}=1\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\) as well.

Example E.1 (Convolution) Figure E.7 shows an animated example for the convolution of the signal \(x(t)\) and the impulse response \(h(t)\) defined as follows: \[x(t) = 1\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\cdot\mathrm{rect}\left(\frac{t}{\mathrm{s}}-\frac{1}{2}\right)\] and \[h(t) = \frac{1\,\mathrm{s}^{-1}}{2} \left(\mathrm{rect}\left(\frac{t}{\mathrm{s}}-\frac{3}{2}\right)-\mathrm{rect}\left(\frac{t}{\mathrm{s}}-\frac{1}{2}\right)\right).\]

Figure E.7: Animated example of the convolution of a signal \(x(t)\) with the impulse response \(h(t)\)

The properties of convolution are summarised in Theorem E.4 (Papula 2014).

Theorem E.4 (Properties of Convolution) With \(x_1(t)\), \(x_2(t)\), and \(x_3(t)\) being complex functions and \(\alpha\in\mathbb{C}\):

  • Commutativity: \(x_1(t) * x_2(t) = x_2(t) * x_1(t)\)

  • Associativity: \(x_1(t) * (x_2(t) * x_3(t)) = (x_1(t) * x_2(t)) * x_3(t)\)

  • Distributivity: \(x_1(t) * (x_2(t) + x_3(t)) = x_1(t) * x_2(t) + x_1(t) * x_3(t)\)

  • Scalar Multiplication: \(\alpha (x_1(t) * x_2(t)) = (\alpha x_1(t)) * x_2(t) = x_1(t) * (\alpha x_2(t))\)

  • Unit Element: \(x_1(t) * \delta(t) = x_1(t)\)

  • Complex Conjugate: \((x_1(t) * x_2(t))^*=x_1^*(t) * x_2^*(t)\)

  • Fourier Transform: (with \(x_1(t) \leftrightarrow X_1(f)\) and \(x_2(t) \leftrightarrow X_2(f)\))

    • \(x_1(t) * x_2(t) \leftrightarrow X_1(f)\cdot X_2(f)\)
    • \(x_1(t) \cdot x_2(t) \leftrightarrow X_1(f) * X_2(f)\)

E.4 Probability Theory

E.4.1 Random Experiment

We consider a random experiment (German: Zufallsexperiment) with outcomes (German: Ereignisse) \(\omega\in\Omega\) occuring with a certain probability \(P(\omega)\). Here, \(\Omega\) denotes the set of possible outcomes (German: Ereignismenge) and \(P\) a probability measure (German: Wahrscheinlichkeitsmaß).

Example E.2 (Tossing a Fair Coin) One popular random experiment is tossing a fair coin. In this case, the set of possible outcomes reads as \[\Omega=\{\text{“Head"},\text{“Tail"}\},\] and the probability of each outcome computes as \[P(\text{“Head"})=P(\text{“Tail"})=\frac{1}{2}.\] This experiment is very related to digital communications where bits element of the set \(\{0,1\}\) are transmitted over a channel. At the receiver, where one does not know if a “0” or a “1” has been transmitted, the transmitted bit can be modeled as a random experiment similar to the tossing of a coin.

E.4.2 Random Variable

Definition E.4 (Random Variable) A random variable \(X\) (German: Zufallsvariable) is a measurable function from a set of possible outcomes \(\Omega\) to a measurable space \({\cal X}\), e.g., the set \(\mathbb{R}\) of real-valued numbers, i.e.,57 \[X:\ \Omega\to {\cal X},\ \omega\mapsto X(\omega).\] The outcome \(x\) of the random variable \(X\) is denoted as a sample (German: Stichprobe or Realisierung).58

A random variable is discrete if \({\cal X}\) is finite or countably infinite59 and continuous if \({\cal X}\) is uncountably infinite60.

Example E.3 (Rolling a Fair Six-Sided Die) Let us consider the rolling of a fair six-sided die. In this case, the set of possible outcomes reads as \[\begin{aligned}\Omega=\{&\text{“1 Auge"},\text{“2 Augen"},\text{“3 Augen"},\text{“4 Augen"},\\ &\text{“5 Augen"},\text{“6 Augen"}\}, \end{aligned}\] and the most obvious choice of the random variable \(X\) is to use the number of pips (German: Augenzahl) as depicted in Table E.2. The random variable \(X\) is discrete in this case.

Table E.2: Random variable for rolling a fair six-sided die
\(\omega\) “1 A.” “2 A.” “3 A.” “4 A.” “5 A.” “6 A.”
\(X(\omega)\) 1 2 3 4 5 6
If the die is fair, i.e., if it is a so-called Laplace die, the probability of each outcome is the same and computes as \[\begin{aligned} P(X=1)&=P(X=2)=P(X=3)=P(X=4)\\ &=P(X=5)=P(X=6)=\frac{1}{6}. \end{aligned}\]

If not stated otherwise, we assume in the following that the random variable \(X\) is real-valued, i.e., \({\cal X}=\mathbb{R}\).

E.4.3 Cumulative Distribution Function

Definition E.5 (Cumulative Distribution Function, CDF) The Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) \(F_X(x)\) (German: Wahrscheinlichkeitsverteilungsfunktion) is defined as \[F_X(x)=P(X\leq x),\] i.e., the probability that samples of the random variable \(X\) are equal or less than the value \(x\). With the CDF, one can compute the probability that samples of a random variable \(X\) lie between the values \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) as \[P(x_1<X\leq x_2)=F_X(x_2)-F_X(x_1).\] Besides, it holds that \(F_X(x\to-\infty)=0\) and \(F_X(x\to \infty)=1\).
Example E.4 (CDF of a Fair Six-Sided Die) The CDF of a fair six-sided die denotes the probability that the number of pips is equal or less to a certain number, i.e., \[F_X(x)=P(X\leq x)=\sum_{\xi=1}^{x} P(X=\xi).\] For example, in case of \(x=2\), we get \[F_X(2)=P(X\leq 2)=P(X=1)+P(X=2)=\frac{2}{6}=\frac{1}{3},\] and if \(x=3.5\), then \[\begin{aligned}F_X(3.5)&=P(X\leq 3.5)\\ &=P(X=1)+P(X=2)+P(X=3)\\ &=3\frac{1}{6}=\frac{1}{2}. \end{aligned}\] For arbitrary \(x\), the CDF of a fair six-sided die reads as \[F_X(x)=\begin{cases} 0,& x<1,\\ \frac{1}{6},&1\leq x < 2,\\ \frac{2}{6},&2\leq x < 3,\\ \frac{3}{6},&3\leq x < 4,\\ \frac{4}{6},&4\leq x < 5,\\ \frac{5}{6},&5\leq x < 6,\\ 1,&6\leq x,\\ \end{cases}\] and is visualised in Figure E.8.

Figure E.8: CDF of a fair six-sided die

If we are interested in the probability that samples of \(X\) lie between two values, e.g., \(x_1=1.5\) and \(x_2=3.5\), we use the CDF in the following way \[\begin{aligned}P(1.5< X \leq 3.5)&=F_X(3.5)-F_X(1.5)\\ &=P(X=2)+P(X=3)\\ &=2\frac{1}{6}=\frac{1}{3}. \end{aligned}\]

E.4.4 Probability Density Function

Definition E.6 (Probability Density Function, PDF) The Probability Density Function (PDF) \(f_X(x)\) (German: Wahrscheinlichkeitsdichtefunktion) is defined as \[f_X(x)=\frac{dF_X(x)}{dx}.\] Again, with the PDF, one can compute the probability that the samples of a random variable \(X\) lie between \(x_1\) and \(x_2\). In this case, it holds that \[P(x_1\leq X\leq x_2)=\int\limits_{x_1}^{x_2}f_X(x)dx.\] Besides, \(\int_{-\infty}^\infty f_X(x)dx=1\), because the probability that samples of \(X\) being any real number is one. If \(X\) has the unit \(1\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\), \(f_X(x)\) has the unit \(1/\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\).
Example E.5 (PDF of a Fair Six-Sided Die) Since the random variable corresponding to the rolling of a six-sided die is discrete, the corresponding PDF is a sum of Dirac delta functions. It reads as \[\begin{aligned}f_X(x)=&\frac{1}{6}\delta(x-1)+\frac{1}{6}\delta(x-2)+\frac{1}{6}\delta(x-3)+\frac{1}{6}\delta(x-4)\\ &+\frac{1}{6}\delta(x-5)+\frac{1}{6}\delta(x-6),\end{aligned}\] and is depicted in Figure E.9.

Figure E.9: PDF of a fair six-sided die

Definition E.7 (Gaussian or Normal Distribution) The Gaussian or normal distribution (German: Gaussverteilung or Normalverteilung) is defined via the following PDF \[f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma_X\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{\left(x-\mu_X\right)^2}{2\sigma_X^2}},\] with the mean \(\mu_X\) and the standard deviation \(\sigma_X\) or variance \(\sigma_X^2\) as explained in the following two sections. If the random variable \(X\) follows a Gaussian distribution with \(\mu_X\) and \(\sigma_X^2\), we write \[X\sim {\cal N}\left(\mu_X,\sigma_X^2\right).\]

The PDF \(f_X(x)\) of a random variable with a Gaussian distribution is depicted in Figure E.10.

Figure E.10: PDF of a random variable with a Gaussian distribution

Example E.6 (PDF of a Noisy Constant Signal Value) Figure E.11 shows the PDF of the signal value \(X=2\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}+N\) where \(N\sim {\cal N}(0,0.5\,\mathrm{W})\) models zero-mean noise. In other words, the random variable \(X\) follows a Gaussian distribution with mean \(\mu_X=2\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\) and variance \(\sigma_X^2=.5\,\mathrm{W}\), i.e., \(X\sim {\cal N}(2\sqrt{\mathrm{W}},.5\,\mathrm{W})\).

E.4.5 Mean

Definition E.8 (Mean) The mean \(\mu_X\) (German: Mittelwert) or the expectation \(E[X]\) (German: Erwartungswert) of a random variable \(X\) is defined as \[\begin{equation} \mu_X=E[X]=\int\limits_{-\infty}^\infty x f_X(x) dx. \tag{E.3} \end{equation}\] In case of a discrete random variable \(X:\Omega\to{\cal X}\), Eq. (E.3) simplifies to \[\begin{equation} \mu_X=E[X]=\sum_{x\in{\cal X}} x P(X=x). \tag{E.4} \end{equation}\]
Example E.7 (Mean of a Fair Six-Sided Die) With Eq. (E.4), the mean number of pips when rolling a fair six-sided die computes to \[\mu_X=\sum_{x=1}^6 x P(X=x)=\frac{1}{6}\sum_{x=1}^6 x=3.5.\]
Example E.8 (Mean of a Random Variable With a Gaussian Distribution) With Eq. (E.3), the mean of a Gaussian distribution computes to \[\int\limits_{-\infty}^\infty x f_X(x) dx= \int\limits_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{x}{\sigma_X\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{\left(x-\mu_X\right)^2}{2\sigma_X^2}} dx =\mu_X,\] showing that the parameter \(\mu_X\) of the Gaussian distribution is exactly its mean.

If we have only access to \(N\) samples \(x_i\), \(i\in\{1,2,\dots,N\}\), of a random variable \(X\), e.g., via Monte Carlo simulations or via performing \(N\) times the random experiment, and do not know the exact PDF, it is not possible to compute the mean \(\mu_X\) analytically. However, an estimation of \(\mu_X\) can be computed as the sample mean or empirical mean \(\hat{\mu}_X\) (German: Stichprobenmittel or empirischer Mittelwert) according to \[\hat{\mu}_X=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N x_i\overset{N\to\infty}{\to}\mu_X.\]

E.4.6 Variance

Definition E.9 (Variance) The variance \(\sigma_X^2\) (German: Varianz) of a random variable \(X\) equals the squared standard deviation \(\sigma_X\) (German: Standardabweichung) and is defined as61 \[\begin{equation} \sigma_X^2=E\left[|X-\mu_X|^2\right]=\int\limits_{-\infty}^\infty |x-\mu_X|^2 f_X(x) dx. \tag{E.5} \end{equation}\] The variance is a measure for the deviation of the random variable \(X\) from the mean \(\mu_X\).

In case of a discrete random variable \(X:\Omega\to{\cal X}\), Eq. (E.5) simplifies to \[\begin{equation} \sigma_X^2=E\left[|X-\mu_X|^2\right]=\sum_{x\in{\cal X}} |x-\mu_X|^2 P(X=x). \tag{E.6} \end{equation}\]
Example E.9 (Variance of a Fair Six-Sided Die) With Eq. (E.6), the variance of the number of pips when rolling a fair six-sided die computes to \[\begin{aligned} \sigma_X^2&=\sum_{x=1}^6 (x-\mu_X)^2 P(X=x)\\ &=\frac{1}{6}\left((1-3.5)^2+(2-3.5)^2+(3-3.5)^2+(4-3.5)^2\right.\\ &\phantom{=}\left.+(5-3.5)^2+(6-3.5)^2\right) \approx 2.917 \approx (1.708)^2, \end{aligned}\] and the standard deviation to \[\sigma_X\approx 1.708.\]
Example E.10 (Variance of a Random Variable With a Gaussian Distribution) With Eq. (E.5), the variance of a Gaussian distribution computes to \[\int\limits_{-\infty}^\infty \left(x-\mu_X\right)^2 f_X(x) dx= \int\limits_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{\left(x-\mu_X\right)^2}{\sigma_X\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{\left(x-\mu_X\right)^2}{2\sigma_X^2}} dx =\sigma_X^2,\] showing that the parameter \(\sigma_X^2\) of the Gaussian distribution is exactly its variance.

Again, if we have only access to \(N\) samples \(x_i\), \(i\in\{1,2,\dots,N\}\), of a random variable \(X\), e.g., via Monte Carlo simulations or via performing \(N\) times the random experiment, and do not know the exact PDF, it is not possible to compute the variance \(\sigma_X^2\) analytically. However, an estimation of \(\sigma_X^2\) can be computed as the sample variance or empirical variance \(\hat{\sigma}_X^2\) (German: Stichprobenvarianz or empirische Varianz) according to62 \[\hat{\sigma}_X^2=\frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^N |x_i-\hat{\mu}_X|^2\overset{N\to\infty}{\to}\sigma_X^2.\]

E.4.7 Random Process

Definition E.10 (Random Process) A time-dependent random variable is denoted as a stochastic or random process \(X(t)\) (German: stochastischer or Zufallsprozess).63 \[X:\ \Omega\times \mathbb{R} \to {\cal X},\ (\omega,t/\mathrm{s})\mapsto X(\omega,t)\] The outcome \(x(t)\) of the random process \(X(t)\) is denoted as a sample function or realisation (German: Stichprobenfunktion oder Realisierung).

E.4.8 Ensemble and Time Average

Definition E.11 (Ensemble and Time Average) We define the ensemble average \(\mu_X(t)\) (German: Scharmittelwert, in general time-dependent) to be \[\mu_X(t)=E[X(t)],\] and the time average \(\overline{x_i}\) (German: Zeitmittelwert, in general dependent on the specific realisation) as \[\overline{x_i}=\lim_{T\to\infty}\frac{1}{T}\int\limits_{-\frac{T}{2}}^\frac{T}{2} x_i(t)dt.\]

In case that the statistical properties of the random process are not known and that a realisation is not available for all \(t/\mathrm{s}\in\mathbb{R}\), the empirical ensemble average (German: empirischer Scharmittelwert) \[\hat{\mu}_X(t)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} x_i(t),\] can be used to obtain an estimate of \(\mu_X(t)\), and the empirical time average (German: empirischer Zeitmittelwert) \[\hat{\overline{x_i}}=\frac{1}{T}\int\limits_{0}^T x_i(t)dt,\] to obtain an estimate of \(\overline{x_i}\). Here, \(N\) is the number of available realisations \(x_i(t)\), \(i\in\{1,2,\dots,N\}\), whose values are given for the time intervall \(t\in[0,T]\).

Example E.12 (Empirical Ensemble and Time Averages of a Band-Limited Noise Signal) Figure E.13 shows the empirical ensemble and time averages of a band-limited noise signal \(X(t)\) with the mean \(\mu_X=0.5\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\) and the variance \(\sigma_X^2=40\,\mathrm{mW}\). \(N=1000\) realisations have been considered for the estimate \(\hat{\mu}_X(t)\) and the time intervall \([0,10\,\mathrm{ms}]\) for the estimates \(\hat{\overline{x_i}}\).

Figure E.13: Empirical ensemble and time averages of a band-limited noise signal

E.4.9 Stationarity and Ergodicity

Definition E.12 (Stationary Process) A random process \(X(t)\) is defined as stationary (German: stationär) if the statistical properties do not depend on the time \(t\), i.e., e.g., in case of a Gaussian distribution, the ensemble average can be simplified to \[\mu_X(t)=\mu_X,\] and the standard deviation \(\sigma_X(t)\) to \[\sigma_X(t)=\sigma_X.\]
Example E.13 (Stationary and Non-Stationary Processes) An example of a stationary process is the noise voltage at a resistor with a certain temperature. However, if the temperature of the resistor changes over time, the random process is no longer stationary.

Stationarity is a required key assumption because in most cases, we do not have access to different realisations of a random process. If we record, e.g., the noise signal of a resistor beginning at one time instance, it is not possible to get another realisation beginning from the same time instance without time traveling or the concept of multiple universes. However, if the statistical properties do not depend on the time, we can simply obtain another realisation by recording the noise signal beginning at a different time instance, e.g., once the first recording has been finished. Cleary, this would not be possible, if the statistical properties of the noise signal change over time.

Definition E.13 (Ergodic Process) A stationary random process \(X(t)\) is defined as ergodic (German: ergodisch) if the ensemble average equals the time average, i.e,64 \[\mu_X=\overline{x}.\]

Example E.14 (Stationarity and Ergodicity of Band-Limited Noise Signal) Remember Example E.12 with Figure E.13. It can be seen that the empirical ensemble average \(\hat{\mu}_X(t)\) is approximately independent of \(t\), i.e., \[\hat{\mu}_X(t)\approx \mu_X = 0.5\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}.\] In other words, the band-limited noise signal is stationary if we assume that all other statistical properties do also not change over time.

Besides, the empirical time averages \(\hat{\overline{x_i}}\) of all realisations are approximately identical, i.e., \[\hat{\overline{x_i}}\approx \overline{x} = 0.5\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}.\]

Moreover and most importantly, the empirical ensemble average is approximately equal to the empirical time average, i.e., it seems that the underlying random process is not only stationary but also ergodic.

Again, ergodicity is a key requirement if we have only access to one realisation. Therefore, throughout this textbook, we only consider ergodic (and stationary) processes!

E.4.10 Auto-Correlation Function

Definition E.14 (Auto-Correlation Function) The auto-correlation function \(\varphi_X(\tau)\) (German: Autokorrelationsfunktion) of an ergodic random process \(X(t)\) with the specific realisation \(x(t)\) computes as65 \[\varphi_X(\tau)=E[X(t)X^*(t+\tau)]= \lim_{T\to\infty}\frac{1}{T}\int\limits_{-\frac{T}{2}}^\frac{T}{2} x(t)x^*(t+\tau)dt.\] The auto-correlation function is a measure for the correlation between the random process at time \(t\) and at time \(t+\tau\). If \(X(t)\) has the unit \(1\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\), \(\varphi_X(\tau)\) has the unit \(1\,\mathrm{W}.\)

E.4.11 Power Spectral Density

Definition E.15 (Power Spectral Density, PSD) The power spectral density \(\Phi_X(f)\) (German: Leistungsdichtespektrum) of an ergodic random process \(X(t)\) is the Fourier transform of its auto-correlation function, i.e., \[\Phi_X(f)\leftrightarrow \varphi_X(\tau).\] The power spectral density paves the way for spectral investigations of random signals for which no Fourier transform is given. If \(X(t)\) has the unit \(1\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\), \(\Phi_X(f)\) has the unit \(1\,\frac{\mathrm{W}}{\text{Hz}}.\)
Example E.15 (PSD Estimate of a Band-Limited Noise Signal) See Example 1.3 for an example of a PSD estimate of a band-limited noise signal. There, the Python function welch from the library scipy.signal has been used to compute the PSD estimate.

Based on the power spectral density \(\Phi_X(f)\), the power of the corresponding signal \(X(t)\) can be computed as \[P_X=\int\limits_{-\infty}^\infty \Phi_X(f) df.\]

In Appendix E.3, we have seen that the deterministic output \(y(t)\) of a deterministic LTI system can be written as \[y(t) = h(t) * x(t) \leftrightarrow Y(f) = H(f)\cdot X(f),\] where \(x(t)\) is the deterministic input signal. The system is represented via its impulse response \(h(t)\) or frequency response \(H(f)\).

If \(X(t)\) and \(Y(t)\) are random processes, this relationship no longer holds because there exists no Fourier transforms in this case. However, with the PSDs \(\Phi_X(f)\) and \(\Phi_Y(f)\) of the processes \(X(t)\) and \(Y(t)\), respectively, it holds \[\begin{equation} Y(t) = h(t) * X(t) \leftrightarrow \Phi_Y(f) = |H(f)|^2\cdot \Phi_X(f). \tag{E.7} \end{equation}\]
