Chapter 5 Passband Communications

Compared to baseband communications which we introduced in Chapter 3, the use of a carrier signal results in passband communications (German: Bandpassübertragung) with a spectrum from \(f_\mathrm{min}>0\) to \(f_\mathrm{max}>f_\mathrm{min}\), i.e., a bandwidth of \(B=f_\mathrm{max}-f_\mathrm{min}\) (see also Definition 2.4). The carrier signal has usually a frequency at the center of the spectrum, i.e., at \(f_0=(f_\mathrm{min}+f_\mathrm{max})/2\). Passband communications is required where multiple communications systems coexist with non-overlapping spectra, e.g., the landline system in the baseband and DSL in a passband on the same wire. Besides, passband communications enables the use of a frequency range which is more approriate for electromagnetic wave propagation, thus, paving the way for wireless communications. Examples of such systems are mobile phone networks (LTE, NR) and broadcast radio.

In this chapter, we will derive a baseband representation of passband communications, helping us to exploit all findings of Chapters 3 and 4, and learn how to modulate and demodulate passband signals. This chapter introduces passband communications in general and specific examples can be found in Chapter 6.

5.1 Equivalent Baseband Signal

Passband signals are represented by equivalent baseband signals. This way, one can exploit most of the findings which we have already derived in the previous chapters for baseband signals. However, the difference to the previous chapters is that the equivalent baseband signal is generally complex-valued.

Definition 5.1 (Equivalent Baseband Signal) The in general complex-valued equivalent baseband signal \(s_\mathrm{EB}(t)/\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\in\mathbb{C}\) (German: äquivalentes Basisbandsignal) of a real-valued passband signal \(s(t)/\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\in\mathbb{R}\) is defined such that \[s(t) = \mathrm{Re}\left\{s_\text{EB}(t)e^{j2\pi f_0 t}\right\}, \] where \(f_0\) denotes the carrier frequency (German: Trägerfrequenz).

Note that there exists no complex-valued signal in physical systems. The equivalent baseband signal is a mathematical representation of the real-valued passband signal, similar to the complex-valued representation of alternating voltages and currents in electrical engineering (German: komplexe Wechselstromrechnung). However, this complex-valued representation helps us to derive a device which generates or modulates passpand signals in Section 5.2 and one which demodulates them in Section 5.3.

In order to gain more insights, we investigate the spectrum of an equivalent baseband signal next. With the properties of the Fourier transform (see Theorem E.1) and \(x(t)\leftrightarrow X(f)\), the real part operation has the following effects on the spectrum: \[\mathrm{Re}\{x(t)\}=\frac{1}{2}x(t)+\frac{1}{2}x^*(t)\leftrightarrow \frac{1}{2}\left(X(f)+X^*(-f)\right).\] Besides, a translation in the frequency domain corresponds to a phase term in the time domain as follows: \[x(t)=s_\mathrm{EB}(t)e^{j2\pi f_0 t} \leftrightarrow X(f)=S_\mathrm{EB}(f-f_0),\] where \(s_\mathrm{EB}(t)\leftrightarrow S_\mathrm{EB}(f)\). Consequently, the spectrum \(S(f)\) of the passband signal \(s(t) = \mathrm{Re}\left\{s_\text{EB}(t)e^{j2\pi f_0 t}\right\}\) computes as \[s(t)\leftrightarrow S(f)=\frac{1}{2}\left[ S_\text{EB}(f-f_0)+S_\text{EB}^*(-f-f_0)\right].\]

Figure 5.1 depicts a spectrum example of the passband and the equivalent baseband signal where the real and imaginary parts are plotted separately.

Figure 5.1: Example of a passband spectrum \(S(f)\) and the equivalent baseband spectrum \(S_\mathrm{EB}(f)\)

Since the passband signal \(s(t)\) is assumed to be real-valued, it holds that \(S(f)=S^*(-f)\) (see symmetry in Theorem E.1). This property has the following consequences:

  • the real part \(\mathrm{Re}\{S(f)\}\) and the absolute value \(|S(f)|\) is even or axially symmetric (German: achensymmetrisch) to the ordinate (German: Ordinate or Hochwertachse) and
  • the imaginary part \(\mathrm{Im}\{S(f)\}\) and the phase \(\angle S(f)\) uneven or centrally symmetric (German: punktsymmetrisch) to the origin (German: Ursprung).

Note that, this symmetry holds only for the passband signal \(s(t)\) since the equivalent baseband signal \(s_\mathrm{EB}(t)\) is generally complex-valued.

Before deriving transceiver components for generating or detecting passband signals based on the equivalent baseband signal in the next chapters, we investigate the special case where the equivalent baseband signal is real-valued, i.e., \(s_\text{EB}(t)/\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\in\mathbb{R}\). In this case, the general formula \[\begin{aligned} s(t)&=\mathrm{Re}\left\{s_\text{EB}(t)e^{j2\pi f_0 t}\right\}\\ &=\mathrm{Re}\left\{s_\text{EB}(t)(\cos(2\pi f_0 t)+j\sin(2\pi f_0 t))\right\},\end{aligned}\] simplifies to \[s(t)=s_\mathrm{EB}(t)\cos(2\pi f_0 t).\] In other words, the passband signal in case of a real-valued equivalent baseband signal is obtained by choosing the amplitude of the carrier signal \(\cos(2\pi f_0 t)\) to be the time-varying baseband signal \(s_\mathrm{EB}(t)\), also denoted as Amplitude Modulation (AM, German: Amplitudenmodulation).

5.2 Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM)

In this section, we derive a transmitter component which transforms the equivalent baseband signal to a passband signal which is finally transmitted over the communications channel. Remember that the equivalent baseband signal \(s_\text{EB}(t)\) is generally complex-valued, i.e., \[\frac{s_\text{EB}(t)}{\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}}=\frac{s_\text{I}(t)}{\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}}+j\frac{s_\text{Q}(t)}{\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}}\in\mathbb{C},\] where the real part \(s_\text{I}(t)/\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\in\mathbb{R}\), also denoted as inphase component (German: Inphasekomponente), as well as the imaginary part \(s_\text{Q}(t)/\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}\in\mathbb{R}\), also denoted as quadrature compononent (German: Quadraturkomponente), are both real-valued, i.e., they are baseband signals as defined in Chapter 3. It follows further: \[\begin{aligned} s(t)&=\mathrm{Re}\left\{s_\text{EB}(t)e^{j2\pi f_0 t}\right\}\\ &=\mathrm{Re}\left\{s_\text{EB}(t)(\cos(2\pi f_0 t)+j\sin(2\pi f_0 t))\right\}\\ &=\mathrm{Re}\left\{(s_\text{I}(t)+js_\text{Q}(t))(\cos(2\pi f_0 t)+j\sin(2\pi f_0 t))\right\}\\ &=s_\text{I}(t)\cos(2\pi f_0 t)- s_\text{Q}(t)\sin(2\pi f_0 t). \end{aligned}\] In other words, the passband signal is constructed by two carrier signals with the same frequency \(f_0\) but having a phase shift of \(\pi/2\) to each other. These two carrier signals are \(\cos(2\pi f_0 t)\) and \(-\sin(2\pi f_0 t)\) and are orthogonal to each other because \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \cos(2\pi f_0 t)\sin(2\pi f_0 t)dt=0\), i.e., information on the inphase component \(s_\mathrm{I}(t)\) does not disturb information on the quadrature component \(s_\mathrm{Q}(t)\) and vice versa. This way, two real-valued baseband signals can be transmitted simultaneously on one passband signal. The resulting modulation is denoted as quadrature amplitude modulation and is summarised in Theorem 5.1.

Theorem 5.1 (Quadrature Amplitude Modulation, QAM) The real-valued passband signal of Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM, German: Quadraturamplitudenmodulation) reads as \[s(t)=s_\text{I}(t)\cos(2\pi f_0 t)- s_\text{Q}(t)\sin(2\pi f_0 t),\] with the carrier frequency \(f_0\) of the Local Oscillator (LO) (German: Lokaloszillator) and the real-valued inphase and quadrature components \(s_\text{I}(t)\) and \(s_\text{Q}(t)\), respectively.

The block diagram of QAM is depicted in Figure 5.2. Here, \(s_\text{I}(t)\) and \(s_\text{Q}(t)\) can be arbitrary baseband signal of Chapter 3. Specific examples will follow in Chapter 6.

5.3 Quadrature Amplitude Demodulation (QAD)

In this chapter, we are deriving a way to extract the signals \(s_\text{I}(t)\) and \(s_\text{Q}(t)\) from the passband signal \(s(t)\), also denoted as demodulation. We assume throughout this chapter that pulse shaping is band-limited to \(B/2\) where \(B\) is the one-sided bandwidth of the passband signal (cf. Definition 2.4).

Let us first consider the demodulation of the inphase component \(s_\text{I}(t)\). If we first multiply the passband signal \(s(t)\) by \(2\cos(2\pi f_0 t)\), we get the following corresponding spectrum (see Theorem E.1 for properties of the Fourier transform and Theorem E.2 for properties of the Dirac) \[s(t)2\cos(2\pi f_0 t)\] \[\updownarrow\] \[\begin{aligned} &S(f)*\left(\delta(f-f_0)+\delta(f+f_0)\right)= S(f-f_0)+S(f+f_0)=\\ &\frac{1}{2}\left[ S_\text{EB}(f-2f_0)+S_\text{EB}^*(-f)+S_\text{EB}(f)+S_\text{EB}^*(-f-2f_0)\right]. \end{aligned}\] Thus, the resulting spectrum consists of four versions of the spectrum \(S_\mathrm{EB}(f)\) of the equivalent baseband signal, two mirrored and complex conjugate versions at \(-2f_0\) and \(0\) and two original versions at \(2f_0\) and \(0\). Since we are only interested in the versions in the baseband, i.e., around the frequency zero, we apply an ideal lowpass filter with a cut-off frequency of \(B/2\), resulting in the spectrum \[\frac{1}{2}\left[S_\text{EB}^*(-f)+S_\text{EB}(f)\right]\] \[\updownarrow\] \[\frac{1}{2}\left(s_\text{EB}(t)+s_\text{EB}^*(t)\right)= \mathrm{Re}\{s_\text{EB}(t)\}=s_\text{I}(t),\] which corresponds to the inphase component \(s_\text{I}(t)\).

Demodulation of the quadrature component is analogous but this time, we multiply the passband signal \(s(t)\) by \(-2\sin(2\pi f_0 t)\). We get \[-s(t)2\sin(2\pi f_0 t)\] \[\updownarrow\] \[\begin{aligned} &S(f)*\left(j\delta(f-f_0)-j\delta(f+f_0)\right)= jS(f-f_0)-jS(f+f_0)=\\ &\frac{j}{2}\left[ S_\text{EB}(f-2f_0)+S_\text{EB}^*(-f)-S_\text{EB}(f)-S_\text{EB}^*(-f-2f_0)\right]. \end{aligned}\] Again, ideal lowpass filtering with a cut-off frequency of \(B/2\), yields the quadrature component \[\frac{j}{2}\left[S_\text{EB}^*(-f)-S_\text{EB}(f)\right]\] \[\updownarrow\] \[\frac{-j}{2}\left(s_\text{EB}(t)-s_\text{EB}^*(t)\right)= \mathrm{Im}\{s_\text{EB}(t)\}=s_\text{Q}(t).\]

Theorem 5.3 and Figure 5.3 summarise the process of demodulation.

Theorem 5.2 (Quadrature Amplitude Demodulation, QAD) Demodulation of the QAM signal, also denoted as Quadrature Amplitude Demodulation (QAD, German: Quadraturamplitudendemodulation) yields \[\begin{aligned} s_\text{I}(t)&=\mathrm{LP}\{s(t)2\cos(2\pi f_0 t)\},\\ s_\text{Q}(t)&=\mathrm{LP}\{-s(t)2\sin(2\pi f_0 t)\}. \end{aligned}\] Here, \(\mathrm{LP}(\cdot)\) denotes ideal lowpass filtering with the cut-off frequency \(B/2\).

5.4 Carrier Synchronisation

In passband communications, it is necessary that the LO of the receiver has exactly the same frequency as the LO at the transmitter. Sometimes, even the same phase is required. This synchrony is obtained by carrier synchronisation (German: Trägersynchronisation) or by carrier recovery (German: Trägerrückgewinnung) where the remainder of the carrier in the received signal is used to synchronize the LO at the receiver with the one at the transmitter.

One famous approach in this context is a Phase-Locked Loop (PLL, German: Phasenregelschleife). A PLL consists of a Voltage Controlled Oscillator (VCO, German: spannungsgesteuerter Oszillator) whose frequency depends on an input voltage. This input voltage is chosen to be a lowpass filtered version of the phase difference between the VCO signal, being the LO of the receiver, and a reference signal which is usually the received signal, i.e., the remaining part of the transmitter’s LO signal. For further details on PLLs see, e.g., Best (2007).

One drawback of the original PLL is that it does not work for so-called suppressed-carrier modulation signals, i.e., signals where the carrier is no longer present. Unfortunately, all phase modulated signals as introduced in Chapter 6 fall in this category. For this type of modulation signals, Costas (1956) introduced the Costas loop (German: Costas-Regelschleife), i.e., a special type of PLL exploiting the carrier as well as a phase shifted version thereof, very similar to the carrier signals of QAM. Further details on the Costas loop can be found in Best (2007).
